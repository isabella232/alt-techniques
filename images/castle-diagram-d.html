The image is taken from an eighteenth-Century engraving in which the various parts of a castle are identified by numbers. Text in the engraving gives a name for each numbered part of the castle.

The castle consists of a square central tower some four or five storeys high, with square turrets at each corner and in the middle of the wall visible in the picture. There is also a flagpole on top of this building, and there are battlements (castellation) around the top of the roof. The entire castle is drawn as if seen from a building slightly taller than this central tower and some distance away, although this is obviously a fictional vantage point. The castle itself is also probably fictional, not representing any specific location.

Surrounding the square tower are three concentric stone walls, also with castellations or arrow-slits at the top of the walls. There are out-buildings in the space between the two smallest rings of wall and a complex gate with turrets on the outermost ring.

The parts are labeled as follows:

1. The Barbacan.

This is a gate with two turrets set entirely outside the main walls; it defends the bridge over moat leading to the outermost wall.

2. The Ditch or Moat.

The ditch runs all the way round the outer stone wall. It is difficult to judge its depth from the picture, but it coulf be anywhere from three feet (one metre) deep to two or three times that depth. It might also have water in it, although again that is not very clear in the picture.

3. Wall of the outer Ballium.

This is the outermost wall, just inside the ditch or (if filled with water) the moat. The wall appears to be a little over two meters (six feet) high and consists of straight segments perhaps twnety feet long joined at angles to make a large rough circle shape. There are towers at most of the corners, some square and some circular. The towers are of varying heights, perhaps between ten and twenty feet, with arrow-slits in the walls around the roof of each tower.

The number 3 is engraved on the diagram just behind the barbican gate (number 1), where an arched entrance in the wall is protected by a circular turret rising up from that same wall, and connects the area inside the outer Ballium with a bridge over the moat leading to the inside of the main entrance, the Barbican.

4. Outer Ballium.

The Outer Ballium is the space between the outermost stone wall and the middle stone wall. The two outwemost walls are perhaps some tenty feet (seven meters) apart from each other. The ground is drawn as flat, using wiggly lines of shading, although on the left-hand side there is a hill.

5. Artificial Mount.

This artificial mont, or mound, is a round hill perhpas ten feet (three meters) high, on the left side of the pictuer, nestling up against the middle wall and almost large enough to reach the outer wall. The walls go upward to account for the height of the mound. There is a square tower built in to the middle wall at the top of the hill.

6. Wall of the Inner Ballium.

The middle wall is labeled in the diagram as the wall of the Inner Ballium; the Inner Ballium is the space between the two innermost walls.

This middle wall is about the same height and nature as the outer wall. It has an arched entrance protected by a circular tower built into the wall. At the right hand end of the wall is a square tower. The wall makes an abrupt right-angled turn at that tower, contuing in its circuit towards the back of the castle. The back left corner of that very rough circle is also a right angle, just like the front right corner, so this middle wall would be somewhat diamond shaped in a plan view.

7. Inner Ballium.

The Inner Ballium is the area between the middle wall and the smallest ring. The two smallest walls are much further apart than the two outermost walls, making the Inner Ballium into a larger space. There are maybe five or six out-buildings in this space, one of which, near the back left, has a tower and might be intended to represent a small church.

8. Keep or Dungeon.

The Keep is the square tower in the center of th diagram. It has a wall going round it that, judging by the part that is visible, is probably six-sided. The wall has towers at each corner and a small entrance-way in the middle of one wall. It is not clear in the diagram wither the entrance is small or whether it has been drawn smaller than the others because it is further away: the artist did not have strong perspective skills.

Neither the innermost wall nor the space between that wall and the Keep are labeled in the diagram.
